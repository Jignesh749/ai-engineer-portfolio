These finance notes cover four core concepts: credit scores, interest rates, loan terms, and basic risk management.

A credit score is a numerical summary of a borrower’s past behavior, usually ranging from poor to excellent. Lenders use credit scores to estimate the probability that a borrower will repay on time. Higher scores generally indicate lower default risk, so borrowers with strong credit histories are more likely to be approved and receive better pricing. Low scores, missed payments, or high existing debt typically lead to higher interest rates or even rejection.

Interest rates represent the cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage of the loan principal. When interest rates are high, the monthly installment and total amount repaid over the life of the loan increase. When rates are low, borrowing becomes cheaper, which can encourage more households and businesses to take loans. Lenders adjust interest rates based on market conditions, the borrower’s risk profile, and the type of product, such as mortgages, personal loans, or credit cards.

Loan terms describe the key contractual details of a borrowing arrangement. The most important elements are the principal (the amount borrowed), the tenure or maturity (how long the borrower has to repay), the repayment schedule (for example, monthly EMIs), and any fees or penalties. Shorter tenures lead to higher monthly payments but lower total interest cost. Longer tenures lower the monthly burden but increase the total amount paid over time.

Basic credit risk management focuses on identifying, measuring, and controlling the risk that borrowers will fail to repay. Common tools include risk-based pricing, where higher-risk borrowers pay higher interest rates, and diversification, where a lender spreads exposure across many different borrowers and sectors. Lenders also use internal rating models, collateral, and regulatory capital rules to ensure they can absorb losses and remain solvent even if some borrowers default.
